INTRODUCTION
We can define motion as the change of position of an object with respect to time
A book falling off a table, water flowing from the tap,rattling windows, etc all exhibit motion.
Even the air that we breathe exhibits motion! Everything in the universe moves. We live in a universe that is incontinual motion.
2.1
Rest and Motion
Motion   - Change in position of an object with respect to its surroundings.
Examples:  
  • Children playing in the ground
  • Birds flying in the sky
  • Cars, Buses and bikes moving on road
  • Movement of earth around sun
Rest   -There is no change in position of an object with respect to its surroundings.

Specific heat capacity of a substance is defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of the substance by 10C or 1 K
SI unit of specific heat capacity is Jkg-1K-1
A high heat capacity means that the substance requires lots of heat in order toraise or lower the temperature by one unit. Having a high heat capacity meansthat the substance also retains heat better than substances with lower heat capacities
A low heat capacity means that the substance does not require a lot of heat inorder to raise/lower the temperature by 1°C. A low heat capacity means that thesubstance does not retain heat as well as substances with a higher heat capacity Formula for Specific Heat Capacity

Uniform circular motion
When an object is moving with a constant speed along a circular path, the velocity changes dueto the change in direction. Hence, it is an accelerated motion.
For example, revolution of earth around the sun,revolution of moon around the earth
If an object, moving along a circular path of radius, r takes time, T to come back to its startingposition, then the speed, v is given by,
Speed = Circumference/Time taken
V = 2pr/T
2.9
Centripetal Acceleration and Centripetal Force
When the acceleration is directed inwards/to the centrein a circular motion, it is known as centripetal acceleration
For an object of mass m, moving along a circular
path of radius r, with a velocity v.
Its centripetal acceleration is given by
a = v2/r
Magnitude (F) = Mass x Centripetal Acceleration
F = mv2/r
2.10
Centrifugal Force
When the acceleration is directed outwards/ away from the centrein a circular motion, it is known as centrifugal acceleration and the associated force is centrifugal force
